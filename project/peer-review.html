Peer Review

A peer review is exactly what it sounds like, a review of your final project by other members of the class. The review will be conducted using a peer review document (available in week 12) which is based on the actual project grading matrix.

Why?

By having another class member look at your project, they may catch errors or find items that are confusing. By providing you feedback on these items it gives you an opportunity to make corrections before the project is actually graded.

When?

Your project should be close to being complete. Therefore, it should be done within 72 hours of when the project is due. That gives you time to make corrections but not so far in advance that the project is not close to being done.

How?

  • Students will work with 2 other members of the class to conduct the reviews.
  • You are required to review the project of at least 2 of your peers and likewise receive at least 2 peer reviews of your own project.
  • Be sure to coordinate between yourselves to ensure that everyone receives the 2 required peer reviews.
  • Use the peer review (available in week 12) to conduct the review of the project.
  • Save the review document using the developer's name and "project review" (e.g. Sally Smith project review.xslx).
  • Email a copy of your review document to the site developer for his/her use.
  • Submit the peer review documents which you conducted (2 total) to the Peer Review dropbox. (Note: These are the reviews you did for other people, not the ones they did for you!)

Grading

  • You will be given 5 points per peer review that you conduct (10 points total). This grading will be done from the submitted reviews in the Peer Review Assignment drop box.
  • If you fail to participate in the peer review process you will lose the 10 points for the two peer reviews AND your dynamic project score will also be penalized 50%! [Yes! This is how important I believe the peer reviews can be!]

Benefit

By having another pair of eyes review your project they will see things that you will not. By finding and pointing out errors, ommissions or confusing operations you can make fixes and better your grade when things are assessed by the professor.
